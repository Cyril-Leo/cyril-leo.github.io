Engagement Ring

To propose to my then girlfriend of 7 years, I made a promise to myself to design and manufacture her engagement ring as a sign of all the growth and happiness I have had with her. Giving myself a three month window, I learned how to solder a ring band, create a stone setting, and polish a ring. I consulted multiple jewelry repair shops and created pratice rings out of sterling silver. For the final ring, I created it out of white gold wire, using white gold solder and a butane braising torch to join all parts together. She said yes, so I now have a project to maintain for the rest of my life

GameCubePC

The GameCube has become a timeless console; however, the technology within has not aged as well as the console. Based on a project done by Youtube channel Tech by Matt, the purpose of this project was to create a portable gaming machine while still maintaining the pleasurable aesthetics of the GameCube. The original project hollowed out the console and added the computer parts, however it lacked actual use of the handle associated with the GameCube. Using the parts from a barebones PC sold by Asrock (the A300 DeskMini) along with a Dremel, soldering iron and a 3D printer, I created a working desktop PC hiding in the shell of a GameCube.

3D Printed Wooden Violin

During the most isolating parts of the pandemic, I decided I would begin to learn a second instrument. The choice I made was the violin. However, the price of a new violin kit was about 120 dollars. At the same time, I learned about the Hovalin, a 3D printable violin, and wood filament, a mixture of wood fibers and PLA that creates a printable material that sets much like real wood, with a lot of the same properties. After adjusting the model for the expansion of the filament and 3 days of printing, the violin was complete. Tuners, strings, and a bow were purchased for this project.

Maze Project

As part of my Computer Aided Manufacturing class for my Manufacturing Minor, I was assigned the task of designing and manufacturing a custom maze out of a 2x3x1 inch piece of aluminum. The maze was designed in Solidworks to fit a 5mm diameter ball bearing on its paths. It was manufactured on a knee and column mill, using face mills, drills, and end mills. The paths for each tool were designed in the Solidworks extension CAMworks.

3D Printed Computer Frame

During the pandemic, as most of my schoolwork went online, I no longer had to access this work outside of my home. Once in person classes began again, I lost the ability to access this work. I decided that instead of carrying a giant tower around, I would construct a server that could run 24/7. I had all the components for this computer except for the case, so I chose to 3D print a custom frame for the parts. I designed it to be able to print as one part, hold MicroATX computer parts, and have a footprint no bigger than the size of the motherboard. The frame itself is made of PLA with threaded brass inserts melted into place to hold the computer parts securely. I can access the machine on any computer or phone using a free remote desktop application called NoMachine.
